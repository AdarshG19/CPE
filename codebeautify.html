When we look at the planets, stars, galaxies, etc., we see that on small scales matter is distributed in an irregular way. But on larger scales (~ 100 mega parsec) it is distributed almost uniformly. Uniformity of cosmic microwave background (CMB) data, which is a relic radiation, predicted by the hot Big Bang model, confirms the homogeneity and isotropy of the universe at large scales. This is otherwise also called Cosmological principle" which states that at any particular time 't', the universe looks similar from all positions in space, and all directions in space at any point are equivalent" (Weinberg 2008, Narlikar 2002).

The standard framework used to study the whole universe as a physical system is Einstein's theory of general relativity (GR). Hubble's discovery that the universe is expanding suggested that it had a beginning - a time when the universe was infinitely dense. There is a considerable amount of evidence showing that most of the mass in the universe is non-luminous and non-interactive referred to as Dark matter. The mass density in the universe, including both luminous and dark matter, has been estimated to be around one third of the total current density. Rest of the energy density of the universe is supposed to be comprised of another mysterious component called Dark energy.

    But the Pythagorean school of thought in ancient Greece held a different view. Its proponents believed reality is fundamentally mathematical.

    More than 2,000 years later, philosophers and physicists are starting to take this idea seriously. Mathematics is an essential component of nature that gives structure to the physical world.

    Why are honeycomb cells hexagonal?

    Honeybees have evolved over time to skillfully build hexagonal honeycomb cells. Why might a hexagon be a suitable shape for storing honey? Could other shapes, such as circles, triangles, or squares, work just as well?

    When you think about it, making circles wouldn’t work too well. It would leave gaps in the honeycomb. The worker bees could use triangles or squares for storage. Those wouldn’t leave gaps. But the hexagon is the strongest, most useful shape.

    Charles Darwin reasoned that bees have evolved to use this shape because it produces the largest cells to store honey for the smallest input of energy to produce wax.

    The honeycomb conjecture states that  a regular hexagonal grid or honeycomb has the least total perimeter of any subdivision of the plane into regions of equal area. The conjecture was proven in 1999 by mathematician Thomas C. Hales.

    Hexagons and honeycomb shapes are also useful for building things humans use, too, like bridges, airplanes, and cars. It gives materials extra strength.

    After all, materials made with hexagon shapes can also handle a lot of force, even if they are made out of a lighter material.
